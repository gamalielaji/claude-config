# International Treaties & Registration Systems — Complete Reference

## 1. Foundation Treaties (Standard-Setting)

### Paris Convention for the Protection of Industrial Property (1883)
- **Scope:** Patents, trademarks, industrial designs, utility models, trade names, GIs, unfair competition
- **Members:** 180 contracting parties
- **Key principles:**
  - National treatment: foreign nationals get same protection as locals
  - Priority right: 12 months for patents/utility models, 6 months for designs/trademarks
  - Independence of patents: patent granted in one country is independent of patents in other countries
  - Right to use trade name without filing or registration
- **Revisions:** Stockholm Act (1967) is current version
- **Administered by:** WIPO

### Berne Convention for the Protection of Literary and Artistic Works (1886)
- **Scope:** Literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works, films, architectural works
- **Members:** 181 contracting parties
- **Key principles:**
  - National treatment
  - Automatic protection: no formalities required (no registration, no notice, no deposit)
  - Independence: protection in each country is independent
  - Minimum standards: life of author + 50 years (most countries exceed)
  - Moral rights: attribution and integrity (Art 6bis)
- **Revisions:** Paris Act (1971) is current version
- **Administered by:** WIPO

### Rome Convention for the Protection of Performers, Producers of Phonograms and Broadcasting Organizations (1961)
- **Scope:** Neighboring/related rights — performers, phonogram producers, broadcasters
- **Members:** 98 contracting parties
- **Key protections:**
  - Performers: consent required for broadcasting/recording live performances (20 years)
  - Phonogram producers: right to reproduce (50 years from fixation)
  - Broadcasters: right to rebroadcast (20 years)
- **Notable non-members:** Indonesia, China (both absent)
- **Administered by:** WIPO, ILO, UNESCO jointly

### TRIPS Agreement (1995)
- **Full name:** Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights
- **Scope:** All IP types — copyright, trademarks, GIs, industrial designs, patents, IC layout designs, trade secrets
- **Members:** 164 (all WTO members)
- **Key features:**
  - Incorporates Paris + Berne standards by reference
  - Adds enforcement obligations (civil, criminal, border measures, provisional measures)
  - Dispute settlement via WTO — enforceable through trade sanctions
  - Transition periods for developing/least-developed countries
  - Doha Declaration (2001): flexibilities for public health (compulsory licensing for medicines)
- **TRIPS-Plus:** Bilateral/regional agreements that exceed TRIPS minimums (e.g., USMCA, CPTPP, RCEP)
- **Administered by:** WTO

### WIPO Copyright Treaty (WCT, 1996)
- **Members:** 116 contracting parties
- **Key additions to Berne:**
  - Computer programs = literary works (Art 4)
  - Databases protectable if creative selection/arrangement (Art 5)
  - Right of "making available to the public" (Art 8) — covers on-demand digital distribution
  - Anti-circumvention: protection against DRM circumvention (Art 11)
  - Rights management information protection (Art 12)
- **Indonesia:** Acceded 2012

### WIPO Performances and Phonograms Treaty (WPPT, 1996)
- **Members:** 114 contracting parties
- **Key provisions:**
  - Moral rights for performers (attribution, integrity)
  - Making-available right for performers and phonogram producers
  - Anti-circumvention and rights management provisions (like WCT)
  - 50-year minimum protection for performers and phonogram producers
- **Indonesia:** Acceded 2012

### Beijing Treaty on Audiovisual Performances (2012)
- **Members:** 50+ contracting parties (entered into force 2020)
- **Key provisions:**
  - Extends performer rights to audiovisual performances (film, TV, video)
  - Moral rights for audiovisual performers
  - Making-available right
  - Transfer of rights provisions

### Marrakesh Treaty to Facilitate Access to Published Works for Persons Who Are Blind, Visually Impaired or Otherwise Print Disabled (2013)
- **Members:** 100+ contracting parties
- **Key provisions:**
  - Mandatory copyright exceptions for accessible format copies
  - Cross-border exchange of accessible format copies
  - Cannot be overridden by contract

### WIPO Treaty on Intellectual Property, Genetic Resources and Associated Traditional Knowledge (2024)
- **Landmark:** First WIPO treaty referencing Indigenous Peoples' rights
- **Key provisions:**
  - Patent applicants must disclose origin of genetic resources used in inventions
  - Disclosure of associated traditional knowledge
  - No new IP rights in genetic resources or TK themselves — defensive measure
- **Significance for Indonesia:** Major biodiversity country with vast traditional knowledge

## 2. Global Registration Systems

### Madrid System (International Trademark Registration)

**Governing instruments:**
- Madrid Agreement (1891) — largely superseded
- Madrid Protocol (1989) — primary instrument
- Common Regulations under the Madrid Agreement and Protocol

**How it works:**
1. Register (or apply for) trademark in home country (Office of Origin)
2. File international application through Office of Origin to WIPO International Bureau
3. WIPO examines formalities, registers in International Register, publishes in WIPO Gazette
4. WIPO notifies designated Contracting Parties
5. Each designated office examines under its national law (12-18 months)
6. Refusal: national office can refuse protection on national grounds
7. Grant: protection equivalent to national registration

**Key features:**
- One application, one language (English, French, or Spanish), one set of fees
- Central attack: if basic mark fails in first 5 years, international registration may be cancelled (but can be "transformed" into national applications)
- Subsequent designation: add countries later without new application
- Central management: renewals, changes, transfers through WIPO
- Scope: 132 countries (115 members covering 132 territories)

**Fees:**
| Component | Amount (CHF) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Basic fee (B&W) | 653 |
| Basic fee (color) | 903 |
| Supplementary fee per class (where applicable) | 100 |
| Individual designation fee | Varies by country ($100-850+) |
| Complementary fee per class (where applicable) | 100 |
| LDC reduction | 90% off basic fee |

**Renewal:** Every 10 years. Basic fee + individual fees for each designated country.

**Limitations:**
- Must have a "basic mark" in Office of Origin
- Central attack dependency (5-year vulnerability)
- Each country still applies own national examination standards
- Not all countries are members (notable: some African countries, some small island states)

### Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT)

**How it works:**
1. File international application (can be at national office or WIPO direct)
2. International search: ISA (International Searching Authority) searches prior art, issues International Search Report (ISR) + Written Opinion
3. Optional: International Preliminary Examination (IPEA) — deeper analysis
4. Publication: 18 months from priority date, application published by WIPO
5. National phase entry: 30 months from priority (most countries). Must enter each desired country individually
6. National/regional examination: each office examines and grants/refuses based on its own law

**Key features:**
- Does NOT grant a "world patent" — delays and centralizes early stages
- Buys time: 30 months (vs. 12-month Paris Convention priority) to decide where to file
- Prior art search: ISR provides early assessment of patentability
- Cost deferral: expensive national phase costs pushed to 30-month mark
- Members: 157+ contracting states

**Fees:**
| Component | Amount (CHF) |
|-----------|-------------|
| International filing fee | 1,330 (reducible by e-filing) |
| Search fee | Varies by ISA ($200-2,000+) |
| Preliminary examination fee | Varies by IPEA |
| National phase fees | Per country ($200-5,000+) |

**Key ISAs for Asian applicants:**
- KIPO (Korean Intellectual Property Office) — popular, high quality, reasonable cost
- JPO (Japan Patent Office) — high quality
- IP Australia — English-language, good for PCT/ASPEC route
- EPO — gold standard, expensive

### Hague System (International Design Registration)

**How it works:**
1. File application directly with WIPO International Bureau
2. WIPO examines formalities, registers, publishes
3. Designated offices examine under national law (6 or 12 months depending on declaration)
4. Protection equivalent to national/regional registration

**Key features:**
- One application can include up to 100 designs (if same Locarno class)
- No basic registration required (unlike Madrid)
- Cost-effective for multi-country design protection
- Members: 97 countries (including EU, Japan, Korea, US, but NOT Indonesia, Malaysia, Thailand, India)

**Fees:**
| Component | Amount (CHF) |
|-----------|-------------|
| Basic fee | 397 (1 design, 1 country. Less for additional designs) |
| Publication fee | 17 per reproduction |
| Individual designation fee | Varies by country |
| Standard designation fee | 42 per design per country |

### Lisbon System (GIs & Appellations of Origin)

**Governing instruments:**
- Lisbon Agreement (1958)
- Geneva Act of the Lisbon Agreement (2015) — expanded to cover GIs beyond appellations of origin

**Members:** 38 contracting parties (relatively small membership)
**Key feature:** International registration of appellations of origin and GIs
**Relevance:** Niche. Important for food, wine, spirits, traditional products

### EU Trademark (EUTM) — Regional System

**Filed at:** EUIPO (Alicante, Spain)
**Coverage:** All 27 EU member states with single registration
**Fees:** 850 EUR (1 class online), +50 EUR (2nd class), +150 EUR (each additional class)
**Duration:** 10 years, renewable
**Opposition period:** 3 months from publication
**Key advantage:** Unitary character — single registration, single enforcement, single renewal
**Key risk:** If challenged successfully in one country, entire EUTM can be affected
**Conversion:** Failed EUTM can be converted to national applications in individual EU states

### Unitary Patent (UP) + Unified Patent Court (UPC)

**Since:** June 2023
**Coverage:** 17+ participating EU states (not all EU members participate)
**Filed through:** EPO (existing European patent application + request for unitary effect)
**Cost:** Lower than validating in multiple countries individually
**UPC:** Single court for patent disputes. Central Division (Paris + Munich), Local/Regional Divisions
**Opt-out:** Holders of existing European patents can opt out of UPC jurisdiction until June 2030

## 3. Classification Systems — Detail

### Nice Classification (Trademarks)
- 45 classes: 34 for goods (Classes 1-34), 11 for services (Classes 35-45)
- Updated annually. 12th Edition currently in force
- Key classes for digital/media companies:
  - Class 9: Downloadable software, apps, digital content, NFTs
  - Class 16: Printed materials, books, magazines
  - Class 25: Clothing, footwear (merchandising)
  - Class 28: Games, toys, playthings
  - Class 35: Advertising, business management, marketing services
  - Class 38: Telecommunications, streaming services
  - Class 41: Entertainment, education, cultural activities
  - Class 42: Software development, SaaS, IT services
  - Class 43: Food/beverage services, restaurants

### Vienna Classification (Figurative Elements)
- Classifies visual elements of trademarks
- Hierarchical: 29 categories → divisions → sections
- Used for searching figurative marks (logos, device marks)

### Locarno Classification (Industrial Designs)
- 32 classes for categorizing industrial designs
- Key classes: Class 14 (recording/communication equipment), Class 32 (graphic symbols/logos/patterns)

### International Patent Classification (IPC)
- Hierarchical classification for patents
- ~70,000 subdivisions
- Section → Class → Subclass → Group → Subgroup
- Key sections for tech: G06 (computing), H04 (electric communication), G16 (healthcare informatics)
