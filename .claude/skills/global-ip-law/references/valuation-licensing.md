# IP Valuation & Licensing

## 1. Valuation Methods — Deep Dive

### 1.1 Income Approach

The most commonly used method for IP valuation. Values IP based on future economic benefits.

**Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)**
- Project future cash flows attributable to the IP
- Apply appropriate discount rate (WACC or risk-adjusted rate)
- Discount to present value
- Key challenge: isolating cash flows attributable specifically to the IP vs. other business assets
- Typical discount rates for IP: 15-35% (higher = more risk)

**Relief from Royalty**
- Estimate what the IP owner would pay to license the IP if they didn't own it
- Use comparable royalty rates from market data
- Apply rate to projected revenues
- Discount to present value
- Most commonly used in litigation and tax disputes
- Formula: IP Value = Sum of (Revenue x Royalty Rate x (1-Tax Rate)) / (1+Discount Rate)^n

**Excess Earnings / Multi-Period Excess Earnings (MPEEM)**
- Calculate total business earnings
- Subtract returns attributable to other assets (working capital, fixed assets, workforce, other intangibles)
- Remaining = earnings attributable to the IP being valued
- Used in purchase price allocation (PPA) under IFRS 3 / ASC 805

**With-and-Without Method**
- Value the business WITH the IP
- Value the business WITHOUT the IP (hypothetical scenario)
- Difference = IP value
- Useful for strategic IP (competitive moat)

### 1.2 Market Approach

**Comparable Transactions**
- Find arm's length transactions involving similar IP
- Adjust for differences (age, quality, market, terms)
- Sources: ktMINE, RoyaltyStat, Bloomberg Law, SEC filings
- Challenge: truly comparable IP transactions are rare

**Comparable Royalty Rates**
- Use industry benchmark royalty rates
- Adjust for specifics of the IP
- More data available than full transaction comparables

### 1.3 Cost Approach

**Reproduction Cost**
- What would it cost to create an exact replica of the IP?
- Includes: R&D, materials, labor, overhead, legal (registration, prosecution)
- Adjustments for obsolescence (functional, technological, economic)

**Replacement Cost**
- What would it cost to create equivalent utility (not exact replica)?
- Generally lower than reproduction cost
- Better reflects market reality

**Limitations:**
- Cost ≠ value. A patent that cost $50K to develop might be worth $50M (or $0)
- Floor estimate only — does not capture market potential
- Best for: early-stage IP, internal accounting, insurance

### 1.4 Qualitative Methods

**IP Scoring / Rating Methods**
Used for portfolio ranking, not precise valuation:
- Technical strength (novelty, breadth of claims)
- Legal strength (prosecution history, validity challenges survived)
- Market relevance (alignment with market needs, competitive position)
- Remaining life (years left on protection)
- Revenue linkage (direct attribution to revenue)
- Score each factor 1-5, weight by importance, produce composite score

### 2.3 The Georgia-Pacific Factors (US Patent Damages)

15 factors US courts consider for reasonable royalty:
1. Existing royalties for the patent
2. Rates paid by licensee for comparable patents
3. Nature and scope of the license (exclusive, territory, restrictions)
4. Licensor's established policy on licensing
5. Commercial relationship between licensor and licensee
6. Effect on licensee's sales of other products
7. Duration of patent and license term
8. Profitability, commercial success, and popularity of the patented product
9. Utility and advantages of the patented product over alternatives
10. Nature of the patented invention
11. Extent of infringer's use of the invention
12. Customary profit or selling price in the industry
13. Portion of profit attributable to the invention vs. unpatented elements
14. Opinion testimony of qualified experts
15. Amount a willing licensor and licensee would agree to (hypothetical negotiation)

## 3. License Agreement Structure

### 3.1 Essential Terms

| Term | Description | Typical Options |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Grant** | What rights are being licensed | Make, use, sell, import, sublicense |
| **Exclusivity** | Exclusive, sole, or non-exclusive | Exclusive commands premium (2-5x non-exclusive) |
| **Territory** | Geographic scope | Worldwide, regional, country-specific |
| **Field of use** | Permitted applications/industries | Unlimited, specific industry, specific product category |
| **Duration** | License term | Fixed term, patent life, renewable |
| **Financial terms** | How licensee pays | Upfront fee, running royalty, minimum guarantee, milestone payments |
| **Sublicensing** | Can licensee grant sublicenses? | Yes/no, with/without consent, revenue share |
| **Improvements** | Who owns improvements? | Grant-back, license-back, independent ownership |
| **Quality control** | Licensor oversight (trademarks) | Mandatory for trademarks (naked license = abandonment) |
| **Termination** | How does the license end? | Breach, convenience, change of control, insolvency |
| **IP warranties** | Licensor's representations | Ownership, non-infringement, validity |
| **Indemnification** | Who bears infringement risk? | Mutual or one-way, caps |
| **Dispute resolution** | How to resolve disputes | Arbitration, mediation, specific court/jurisdiction |

### 3.2 Financial Structures

**Running royalty only:**
- % of net sales/revenue
- Lower upfront cost for licensee
- Aligns incentives (licensor earns when licensee succeeds)
- Risk: low sales = low return for licensor

**Upfront fee + running royalty:**
- Lump sum at signing + ongoing %
- Upfront fee covers licensor's costs and signals commitment
- Running royalty lower than royalty-only deal
- Common in pharma, tech licensing

**Minimum guarantee (MG):**
- Licensee commits to minimum annual payment regardless of sales
- Protects licensor from non-performance
- Excess over MG paid as royalty
- Standard in entertainment/character licensing

**Milestone payments:**
- Triggered by development/regulatory/commercial milestones
- Common in pharma (IND filing, Phase I/II/III, FDA approval, first commercial sale)
- Typical: escalating amounts ($100K → $1M → $10M+)

**Paid-up license:**
- Single lump sum for all rights
- No ongoing payments
- Simpler administration
- Licensor bears risk of undervaluation

**Hybrid/tiered:**
- Royalty rate changes based on volume thresholds
- Example: 5% on first $10M sales, 3% on $10-50M, 2% above $50M
- Incentivizes licensee to maximize sales

### 3.3 Critical Clauses

**Most favored licensee (MFL):**
- If licensor grants better terms to another licensee, first licensee gets same terms
- Common request from licensees
- Licensor: resist or limit scope carefully

**Audit rights:**
- Licensor can audit licensee's books to verify royalty payments
- Typically: annual right, 30-day notice, at licensor's expense (unless underpayment >5%, then licensee pays)

**Change of control:**
- What happens if licensee is acquired?
- Options: automatic termination, consent required, continuation with notice
- Critical for strategic licenses

**Bankruptcy/insolvency:**
- What happens to the license if licensee goes bankrupt?
- Varies by jurisdiction. US: §365(n) Bankruptcy Code protects IP licensees
- Other countries: less clear protection

## 4. IP Monetization Strategies

### 4.1 Revenue Models

| Strategy | How It Works | Best For |
|----------|-------------|----------|
| **Direct licensing** | License IP to third parties for royalties | Strong IP, established market |
| **Cross-licensing** | Exchange licenses with competitors | Patent-dense industries (telecom, semiconductor) |
| **Patent pool** | Multiple patent holders pool for single license | Industry standards (MPEG, Wi-Fi, 5G) |
| **IP sale/assignment** | Sell IP outright | Non-core IP, cash need, portfolio pruning |
| **Spin-off/carve-out** | Create separate entity around IP | High-value IP that deserves focused development |
| **Securitization** | Use IP as collateral for financing | Proven revenue-generating IP |
| **Franchise** | License entire business model (brand + know-how + system) | Replicable business models |
| **Merchandising** | License brand/characters for products | Entertainment, sports, character IP |
| **Technology transfer** | License + know-how + training + support | University tech, manufacturing processes |
| **Litigation/enforcement** | Sue infringers for damages | Clear infringement, strong patents |

### 4.2 IP-Backed Financing

- **IP collateral:** Use registered IP as security for loans. Banks increasingly accepting (especially patents and trademarks)
- **IP securitization:** Bundle IP royalty streams into asset-backed securities. Pioneered by Bowie Bonds (music royalties)
- **IP insurance:** Insure against IP infringement claims or fund enforcement
- **Government IP financing:** Some countries offer IP-backed loans (Singapore, South Korea, Japan)
- **IP valuation for fundraising:** IP value as part of company valuation in equity rounds

### 4.3 IP Tax Optimization

**Patent/IP box regimes:** Reduced tax rate on income from qualifying IP
| Country | Regime | Effective Tax Rate on IP Income |
|---------|--------|-------------------------------|
| Netherlands | Innovation Box | 9% (vs. 25.8% standard) |
| Ireland | Knowledge Development Box | 6.25% (vs. 12.5% standard) |
| UK | Patent Box | 10% (vs. 25% standard) |
| Luxembourg | IP Regime | 5.2% effective |
| Singapore | IP Development Incentive | 5-10% |
| Switzerland | Patent Box (cantonal) | Varies by canton |

**BEPS Action 5:** OECD nexus approach requires substantial activity (R&D) in the jurisdiction claiming IP box benefits. No more "brass plate" IP holding companies.

**Transfer pricing:** Intercompany IP licenses must be at arm's length. OECD Transfer Pricing Guidelines Chapter VI (intangibles). CbCR (Country-by-Country Reporting) requirements.

## 5. IP Accounting Standards

### IFRS (International)
- **IAS 38:** Intangible Assets. Recognition: identifiable, controlled, future economic benefit
- **IFRS 3:** Business Combinations. Purchase price allocation — identify and value all acquired intangibles
- Internally generated intangibles: development costs capitalizable if 6 criteria met. Research costs = expense immediately
- Brands/mastheads/titles: internally generated = generally cannot be capitalized. Acquired = capitalize and amortize or impairment test

### US GAAP
- **ASC 350:** Intangibles — Goodwill and Other. Similar to IAS 38
- **ASC 805:** Business Combinations. Similar to IFRS 3
- Key difference: US GAAP generally more restrictive on capitalizing development costs (most expensed)
- Research = expense. Development = generally expense (except software: ASC 350-40, ASC 985-20)

### Amortization
- Finite-lived intangibles: amortize over useful life (straight-line or accelerated)
- Indefinite-lived (e.g., trademarks maintained indefinitely): no amortization, annual impairment test
- Goodwill: no amortization (IFRS/US GAAP for public companies), annual impairment test
